The hunt for new worlds

Since the first confirmed discovery in 1992, astronomers rely on many exoplanet-hunting techniques. In , they primarily used radial velocity: watching for the subtle wobble of a star pulled by unseen gravity. This changed in 2009 with the dawn of the , which popularised the transit method by staring at thousands of stars and seeking a dip in brightness. , using many more observatories, both ground-based as well as in space, we are finding strange worlds with a variety of techniques.

But where is this data coming from? Early discoveries relied entirely on ground-based telescopes located on remote, high-altitude mountaintops. Hover over the map below to see how famous peaks like in Hawaii or in Chile act as massive hubs for multiple observatories.

However, the modern era is heavily driven by space-based missions orbiting far above Earth's atmospheric distortion, like and . These telescopes are custom-built for the purpose of searching exoplanets using the transit method.

With so many advanced observatories on Earth and in space, you might assume we have mapped the entire cosmos. The map below projects every confirmed exoplanet onto the celestial sphere using Right Ascension (horizontal) and Declination (vertical). You can explore the sky map by scrolling to zoom and dragging to pan, hover over planets to inspect their coordinates and distance to us, and hover over the legend to isolate discovery methods.

While discoveries appear scattered across the map, one feature stands out immediately: the massive, incredibly dense cluster on the top-right. That is the . The Kepler Space Telescope didn't scan the whole night sky; it stared continuously at one single patch of the Milky Way for four years. Also on the right, but below the celestial equator, you'll spot a concentrated patch of green microlensing discoveries. Because a microlensing alignment is rare, astronomers have to look at hundreds of millions of stars simultaneously to catch it happening. The is the only place in the sky with enough background stars tightly packed together to make this method reliably successful.

The sheer volume of planets discovered within the Kepler Field of View and the galactic bulge region is profound. It tells us that our current map is not a complete picture, but a tiny sample. It suggests that billions of worlds remain hidden in the vast, unobserved dark gaps of this map, just waiting to be found.

Planetary systems visualised

We have mapped where these worlds are in the night sky, but what do their actual planetary systems look like? Our own system has eight planets arranged in wide, near-circular orbits, with four rocky planets in the inner solar system nicely separated from four gas giants further away.

The visualisation below maps out (exo)planetary systems. The horizontal axis represents the distance from the host star in astronomical units (AU), plotted on a logarithmic scale to be able to show all planets on one page. The size of each circle reflects the physical radius of the planet.
