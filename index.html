Exoplanets

Exploring distant worlds through data

For ages, people looked up at the stars and thought we were all alone. Today, we know the galaxy is full of exoplanets, which are planets orbiting stars other than our own Sun. It feels like we should have plenty of neighbours. Yet, so far, our own Solar System still looks weirdly unique. This project digs into the data to see if we really are special, or if we’re just limited by the tools we use to look for them.

The dataset

The source of our data is a database of astronomical data and information on exoplanets and their host stars: the NASA Exoplanet Archive. It compiles discoveries from ground-based observatories and space telescopes. Behind every data point is at least one scientific paper confirming the existence of a world beyond our solar system.

Discovery methods

Planets are incredibly faint compared to their host stars. To find them, astronomers rely on ingenious indirect methods, each with its own strengths and limitations. As always, Wikipedia is a good starting place for more information.

Transit

Observing a star's slight dip in brightness as a planet passes directly in front of it.

Radial velocity

Measuring the subtle "wobble" of a star caused by the gravitational pull of an orbiting planet.

Microlensing

Using the gravity of a planetary system to bend and magnify the light of a distant background star.

Direct imaging

Taking an actual picture of the planet by blocking out the overwhelming glare of its host star.

Other methods

Includes rarer techniques like astrometry (measuring physical star movement), pulsar timing, and eclipse timing variations.
